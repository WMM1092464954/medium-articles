Notion AI Review: An Honest Take After Real-World Use in 2026

Six months of daily use, three failed alternatives, and one surprisingly complicated answer

The Hook

It was a Tuesday afternoon in late January when I finally hit my breaking point with fragmented workflows. I had Chrome tabs open to ChatGPT, a separate Obsidian vault, a Trello board, and three Google Docs — all supposedly working together to help me manage a product launch. I was toggling between them like a caffeinated tennis ball.

A colleague casually mentioned she'd consolidated most of that chaos into Notion AI. I was skeptical. I'd used Notion for two years purely as a database tool and dismissed the AI layer as marketing fluff. But desperation makes you humble.

Six months later, I have a genuinely complicated opinion — and that's exactly why I'm writing this. Most reviews of Notion AI are either breathless endorsements or lazy dismissals. Neither helps you make a real decision. This one will.

What Notion AI Claims to Do

Notion AI positions itself as a contextually aware AI assistant built directly into your workspace. The pitch isn't "another ChatGPT wrapper." It's smarter than that. The idea is that the AI already knows your notes, your databases, your project history, and your writing style — so instead of pasting context into a separate tool, the intelligence lives where your work already lives.

Core advertised features include:

  • AI-powered writing, editing, and summarization inside any page
  • Q&A across your entire workspace (the "Ask AI" function that queries your own knowledge base)
  • Database automation with AI-generated properties
  • Meeting note summarization and action item extraction
  • Connected search across integrated tools like Slack and Google Drive

In 2026, they've also rolled out more robust AI agents that can execute multi-step tasks within your workspace. The ambition is clear: replace the AI tab-switching entirely.

My Actual Use Cases

1. Weekly Research Synthesis (High Value)

I run a small consultancy and spend roughly six hours per week consuming industry reports, newsletters, and client briefs. My workflow now: dump raw notes into a Notion database, tag them by project, and use Ask AI to surface patterns across dozens of documents at the end of the week.

Result: What used to take ninety minutes of manual cross-referencing now takes twenty. The summaries aren't perfect — more on that below — but they're accurate enough to be a genuine first draft of insight rather than a blank page problem.

2. Client Proposal Drafting (Mixed Results)

I store past proposals, client context, and pricing templates in Notion. The dream was that AI would help me generate new proposals that felt personalized, not templated.

Reality check: The AI is genuinely good at structuring proposals and pulling in relevant details from linked databases. But the voice consistently drifts toward corporate blandness unless you aggressively edit the prompts. I've learned to use it for skeleton-building and section outlines, then rewrite the actual prose myself. It saves time, but it's a supporting actor, not the lead.

3. Meeting Notes to Action Items (Excellent)

This is where Notion AI genuinely earns its keep. I paste raw meeting transcripts — exported from Otter.ai or Fireflies — directly into Notion pages. One click extracts a summary, key decisions, and a formatted action item table with owners and deadlines.

Result: Near-perfect accuracy on factual extraction. I've run this across roughly forty meetings and can count the significant errors on one hand. This single workflow alone probably saves me three hours per week.

4. Internal Knowledge Base Q&A (Promising but Uneven)

This is the flagship feature I was most excited about and the one with the widest gap between promise and reality. Ask AI can query your workspace — theoretically letting you ask "What did we decide about the pricing model in Q3?" and get a sourced answer.

When it works: Brilliant. Genuinely feels like having a research assistant who read everything.

When it fails: With larger workspaces (mine has about 800 pages), the retrieval can miss obvious documents or hallucinate connections between unrelated notes. I've learned to treat answers as leads to verify, not conclusions to trust blindly.

Feature Breakdown

✅ What Actually Works

In-line editing and rewriting is consistently solid. Asking it to make something "more concise" or "adjust the tone to be more direct" produces useful output 80% of the time. It understands context from the surrounding page better than most standalone AI tools.

Database AI properties are underrated. I have a CRM database where an AI property automatically generates a one-line summary of each client relationship from scattered notes. This updates dynamically and saves me from writing manual summaries for eighty clients.

The writing assistant toolbar integrates so naturally into the editing flow that it stops feeling like a separate tool. That frictionlessness is real and shouldn't be underestimated.

❌ What Doesn't Work

Hallucination in Q&A remains a real problem when your workspace scales. Notion AI doesn't always know what it doesn't know, which is the worst kind of failure for a knowledge management tool.

Complex reasoning chains fall apart. If you ask it to analyze a database, draw a conclusion, then recommend an action based on that conclusion — the logic breaks down by step three. It's not an agent for anything genuinely sophisticated yet, despite the marketing.

Template generation produces output that feels instantly recognizable as AI-generated. It hasn't developed enough nuance to match established brand voices without substantial human reworking.

Vs. The Competition

Notion AI vs. ChatGPT (with custom GPTs)

ChatGPT with a well-built custom GPT is more powerful for pure reasoning and complex writing tasks. There's no question. But it requires you to constantly paste context in, manage files externally, and context-switch. For people who already live in Notion, the integration advantage of Notion AI is worth a meaningful capability tradeoff. For people who don't, it isn't.

Notion AI vs. Obsidian + AI Plugins

Obsidian's AI plugin ecosystem (particularly with local models) offers more privacy and customization for power users. But the setup friction is significant, and it lacks Notion's collaboration features. If you're a solo knowledge worker with strong technical confidence and privacy requirements, Obsidian wins. If you're on a team, Notion isn't close to being beaten here.

Notion AI vs. Microsoft Copilot (in Teams/Office)

For organizations already inside the Microsoft ecosystem, Copilot is a more natural fit and arguably more powerful for document-heavy workflows. Notion AI wins decisively on flexibility, user experience, and database integration. Copilot wins on enterprise compliance, deep Office integration, and email management. These tools are solving for different organizational profiles.

Pricing Reality Check

As of 2026, Notion AI is available as an add-on at $10 per member per month (billed annually) on top of your existing Notion plan. The Plus plan starts at $10/month, meaning a solo user is looking at approximately $20/month all-in.

Is it worth it?

For solo knowledge workers or consultants: Yes, if you're already in Notion. The meeting note extraction alone justifies the cost if your time is worth anything. The Q&A features are valuable enough to keep.

For teams: It depends heavily on adoption. The per-seat cost adds up quickly, and the value is only realized if your workspace is actually well-organized. Notion AI applied to a chaotic workspace produces faster chaos, not clarity.

Honest comparison: $10/month for Notion AI vs. $20/month for ChatGPT Plus. If you're only using one, ChatGPT is more versatile. If you're choosing between them as additions to a Notion workflow, the AI add-on is the better value because of the integration depth.

Who Should Use It / Who Should Avoid It

Use Notion AI if you:

  • Already use Notion daily and have a reasonably organized workspace
  • Process meeting notes, research, or client documentation regularly
  • Work on a small team where shared AI context has compounding value
  • Want AI that reduces context-switching rather than adding another tab

Avoid Notion AI if you:

  • Are brand new to Notion — learn the base tool first, or the AI layer adds confusion
  • Need deep reasoning or complex multi-step AI agents (look at dedicated tools)
  • Have a disorganized workspace — garbage in, garbage out applies brutally here
  • Work in an enterprise environment with strict compliance requirements
  • Are primarily a writer who needs strong voice preservation — the prose quality won't meet your bar

Final Verdict

Notion AI in 2026 is not the AI-everything tool its marketing suggests. It's a genuinely excellent workflow integration tool that dramatically amplifies the value of an already well-maintained Notion workspace.

The meeting summarization is legitimately best-in-class for the price. The database AI properties are an underrated feature that most reviewers overlook. The in-line editing is seamless in a way that standalone tools simply cannot replicate.

But don't buy the vision entirely. The Q&A function needs human verification at scale. The complex reasoning isn't there yet. The writing quality requires a heavy editorial hand for anything client-facing.

My recommendation: If you're a Notion user processing more than five meetings per week or managing a knowledge base across more than one project simultaneously, the $10/month is almost certainly worth it. Run the trial with real work — not toy examples — for two weeks. If the meeting extraction alone doesn't save you time, cancel without guilt.

If you're evaluating Notion because of the AI features, start with the base product. The AI is a multiplier, not a foundation. Build the foundation first.

Six months in, I'm keeping it. Not uncritically — but deliberately.
