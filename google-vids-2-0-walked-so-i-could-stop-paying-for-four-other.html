What Even Is Google Vids 2.0?

If you missed the first version: Google Vids is Google's AI-assisted video creation tool, baked directly into Google Workspace. Version 2.0, which rolled out more broadly in early 2026, added serious muscle — AI script generation, automatic scene assembly from Google Slides decks, voiceover synthesis, stock footage integration via Google's partnership with Getty, and a timeline editor that doesn't make you want to quit your job.

It lives at vids.google.com, looks like a cousin of Google Slides, and costs nothing extra if you're already on a Business Starter plan or above.

That last part is doing a lot of heavy lifting.

Google Vids 2.0 — Best for Workspace-native business video

My Use Case

My day job involves producing internal training videos, product demos, and the occasional executive update. Before this, my stack was: Loom for quick recordings, Descript for editing, Canva for motion graphics, and occasionally Runway when something needed a visual upgrade. That's four separate subscriptions, four separate login headaches, and four separate places where files go to die.

I ran Vids 2.0 through three real projects:

  1. A 6-minute onboarding video for a new product feature
  2. A 3-minute executive summary with data visualizations pulled from Google Sheets
  3. A training module broken into five short clips (~90 seconds each)

Here's what happened.

What I Actually Love

The Slides-to-Video pipeline is genuinely magic. I'm not using that word loosely. I had a 22-slide deck that I'd already built in Google Slides. I clicked "Create video from presentation," watched Vids pull in every slide, auto-suggest scene durations based on text density, and generate a first-draft script in about 40 seconds. The script wasn't perfect — more on that — but the structure was solid. I went from deck to rough-cut video in under 20 minutes. That used to take me two hours minimum when I was building equivalent videos in Descript or CapCut.

For the onboarding video, this alone saved me roughly 90 minutes of work.

The AI voiceover is way better than I expected. Google's text-to-speech has been notoriously robotic for years. Vids 2.0 ships with a new set of voices (labeled as "Workspace Studio Voices") that are noticeably more natural. There's still a ceiling — you can tell it's synthetic if you listen closely — but for internal training content or B2B explainers, it clears the bar. I used "Jordan" (neutral, mid-paced) for all three projects and got zero complaints from stakeholders. One person actually asked if I'd hired a voiceover artist. Real talk: I almost didn't correct them.

Everything lives where your work already lives. This sounds boring until you've spent 20 minutes hunting for a Descript project that somehow ended up in three different export folders. With Vids, your video is in Drive. Your script pulls from Docs. Your data pulls from Sheets. Sharing works exactly like any other Google file — view only, commenter, editor. The collaboration loop with async video review (commenters can drop time-stamped notes) cut my revision cycles by about 40% on the executive summary project because everyone was already in the same ecosystem.

Honest Complaints

The AI script generator plays it very safe. Every first draft it produces sounds like a corporate training video from 2019. "Welcome to this overview of our new feature. In this video, you will learn..." — that kind of energy. I ended up rewriting about 70% of the generated scripts before recording. For someone who writes well, this is a minor annoyance. For someone hoping to skip the writing entirely, it's a real disappointment. The script AI needs a tone setting or at least a "be less beige" toggle. I genuinely hope they ship that.

Timeline editing is functional, not powerful. If you've used Premiere, DaVinci, or even CapCut seriously, Vids 2.0's timeline will feel like riding a bike with training wheels. You can trim clips, reorder scenes, adjust audio levels — basic stuff. But you can't do multicam, can't do proper B-roll layering, can't add custom transitions beyond the three generic ones it offers. For polished external marketing videos, you will hit a wall. I hit it hard on the training module clips when I wanted to add a screen recording layer on top of the stock footage. The answer was basically "you can't do that here."

Who It's For

This tool is built for one person: the business professional who needs to produce decent video content regularly, doesn't have time to learn complex editing software, and is already inside Google Workspace all day. That's a massive audience, and for that audience, it genuinely delivers.

It is not for video editors, filmmakers, marketers building high-production campaigns, or anyone whose content lives primarily outside the Google ecosystem. If you're a HubSpot house or you live in Notion, the integration story falls apart.

Pricing: Included with Google Workspace Business Starter ($6/user/month) and above. No standalone plan currently. If you're paying for Workspace anyway, this is essentially free.

Rating: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (4/5)

I docked one star for the script quality ceiling and the timeline limitations. But four stars is still a strong recommendation for the right person.

The Tools It Replaced (And the One It Didn't)

After eight weeks, here's what changed in my stack:

  • Loom — mostly replaced for anything over 2 minutes. I still use Loom for quick "here's the bug" clips because Vids has too much setup for a 45-second screen capture.
  • Descript — replaced for structured project videos. The Workspace integration beats Descript's workflow for my use case specifically.
  • Canva (video features) — replaced. Vids handles motion and transitions well enough that I don't need Canva for this anymore.
  • Runway — not replaced. If I need serious AI video generation or visual effects work, Runway is still doing things Vids can't touch.

Net result: I'm paying for one fewer subscription. That's not nothing.

The Comparison Nobody Wants to Make But Should

Look, the obvious comparison is Vids 2.0 vs. Runway vs. Loom. But the more honest comparison is Vids 2.0 vs. doing nothing — which is what most people in corporate environments are actually doing. Most business professionals aren't making professional videos. They're sending 40-slide decks with no narration and hoping people read them.

Vids 2.0 lowers that barrier so much that the real win isn't "it's better than Runway." It's "it will actually get used." A video tool you use beats a better video tool you don't.

My Honest Verdict

If you're a Google Workspace shop producing internal or B2B video content — training, product updates, onboarding, exec comms — Google Vids 2.0 is the easiest decision you'll make this year. It's already in your subscription. It connects to everything you're already using. It produces results that are genuinely good enough for 80% of business video needs.

If you're a creative professional, a marketer building polished external content, or someone who needs serious editing control, keep your current stack. Vids 2.0 will frustrate you within the first hour.

The thing I keep coming back to: Google built this for the person who wasn't making videos before. And for that person, it changes the game.

Just plan on rewriting whatever the AI gives you for a script. That part still needs a human.
